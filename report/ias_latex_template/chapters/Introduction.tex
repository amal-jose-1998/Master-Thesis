\chapter{Introduction}

Automation is increasingly influencing nearly every aspect of daily life, and transportation, one
of humanity’s greatest inventions for progress, is also undergoing a drastic transformation. \acp{AV} are at the forefront of this change, revolutionizing the transportation sector and advancing
rapidly with each technological breakthrough. The rapid progress in \ac{AI}, advanced
sensing, and communication technologies has accelerated the development of \acp{AV}. These innovations promise to enhance traffic efficiency, improve travel comfort, increase road
safety, and, most importantly, reshape the future of mobility.

\par \acp{AV} are increasingly deployed in road environments. However, their integration into existing traffic
networks introduces significant complexities, particularly in mixed-traffic environments where they must
coexist and interact with \acp{SAV} and \acp{HDV}. A major
challenge in these scenarios is the presence of asymmetric information. These agents differ not only in
their dynamics but also in the type and quality of information they expose. \acp{AV} typically rely on
rich on-board sensing and potentially \ac{V2X} communication, whereas \acp{HDV} provide
no explicit communication and exhibit strongly stochastic behaviour \cite{Schwarting2018}\cite{abedi2025precisehdvpositioningsafetyaware}. \acp{SAV} form an intermediate class
in which partial automation and limited communication capabilities may be available, while a human driver still retains controll 
authority over the vehicle.

\par This mixture of agents creates a fundamentally asymmetric information setting. The \ac{AV} must reason
about other road users whose internal goals, intentions, and perceptions are only indirectly observable. To
navigate safely and efficiently, the \ac{AV} must infer their likely behaviour, anticipate their responses to its
own actions, and choose manoeuvres that keep risk within acceptable bounds.

\par Over roughly the last two decades, the researchers have developed three broad families of behavioural
models for road users: rule-based models, probabilistic models, and data-driven or learning-based methods \cite{10054035} \cite{Rudenko_2020} \cite{Lefevre2014}.
In parallel, several game-theoretic decision-making paradigms for \acp{AV} have emerged \cite{automotiveinnovation}. For \acp{HDV}, the discussion
highlights the limitations of deterministic and purely data-driven models in this context and motivates
the use of \ac{DBN} as a principled framework capable of representing latent
driver intentions, handling partial observability, and supporting probabilistic prediction. Unlike \ac{HDV}
behaviour modelling, which has matured through rule-based, probabilistic, and learning-based frameworks,
the modelling of \acp{SAV} is far less developed. There is currently no widely accepted
\ac{SAV} behavioural model or trajectory prediction framework.
